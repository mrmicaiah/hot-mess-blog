The bell rings at 2:47 on a Tuesday, which is the part of the afternoon when I'm usually doing one of three things: restocking the sugar caddy (which has been a personal nemesis of mine since approximately story three of my life), wiping down the espresso machine, or staring out the window at the cemetery and thinking about whether I should eat a second muffin. Today it's the sugar caddy. The caddy and I have a long-standing arrangement where I refill it and it dumps itself out the moment I turn my back, and we are currently in a quiet truce.

The bell rings, and I look up from a fistful of raw sugar packets to see a woman pushing through the door like she's been launched.

She's maybe late thirties, blonde from a bottle but a *good* bottle, oversized purse, sunglasses pushed up into her hair even though it's overcast. Her eyes are the soft pink-rimmed kind that say either allergies or crying, and the way she's holding her jaw suggests it's not allergies.

She's at the counter before the bell stops ringing.

"Hi. Hi. Okay. Vanilla latte. Large. Whole milk. Extra shot. Please."

"Sure thing," I say, already reaching for a cup. "For here or to go?"

"Here. I just need to *sit* somewhere that isn't my house, you know? Like I love my house, I picked the paint colors myself, but right now my house is a crime scene because Mark — that's my husband, my *soon-to-be-ex* husband — Mark took the dining room table when he left and I have been eating dinner standing at the kitchen island for *six weeks* and I just — I needed somewhere with chairs."

I have, at this point, written her name on a cup. *Brittany.* She'd given it to me in the same breath as her order, and I'd written it down before I'd entirely registered it, which is how I know my hands are working even when the rest of me is still catching up.

"Chairs are good," I say, because I am a person who, when handed an emotional grenade, will say something like *chairs are good.*

"Right? Right. *Thank you.* See, this is what I'm saying. People don't talk anymore. People are just on their phones or whatever, and meanwhile my whole life is falling apart, and yesterday my contractor — did I mention I'm doing a kitchen remodel? I started it before everything with Mark, and now I'm stuck with it, like it's my *child* now — yesterday my contractor didn't show up. Just didn't show up. No call. No text. Nothing."

I have started the espresso. Two shots. I am also aware that I have not said anything since *chairs are good*, and Brittany does not appear to have noticed.

"And the worst part — the *worst* part — is that Trish, that's Mark's sister, Trish has been texting me like she still wants to be friends, but I know for a *fact* she's been over at his apartment, because Megan from Pilates saw her car there on a Wednesday, which, who goes to your brother's apartment on a *Wednesday* unless something is happening, you know?"

"Mm," I say, which is a sound I make sometimes when I am buying time.

The milk is steaming. I am pouring the latte. I am attempting an actual rosetta because Todd said something one time about how my rosettas are getting better and I have been trying to live up to that compliment in private ever since, like it's a piece of homework.

"And then *Biscuit* — Biscuit's my dog, he's a corgi mix, the *sweetest* — Biscuit has to have this dental procedure that's going to be fourteen hundred dollars, and Mark is supposed to pay half but his lawyer says — *his lawyer*, Rena, his lawyer says — "

I slide the latte across the counter. Beautiful rosetta. I take a moment of private joy in the rosetta.

"I'll let you grab a seat," I say, in the voice I use to politely move someone along, which has never worked once in my life.

"Oh, sure. Sure. Can I get another one? Like, the same. I'm gonna be here a while."

"Sure thing," I say, because of course I do.

---

By the time I'm pulling shots for the second latte, Brittany has not stopped talking, and four people have come in.

Patricia is at the corner table, half-caf oat milk extra hot light foam in front of her, and she has, for the last seven minutes, been watching me the way a cat watches a bird through a window. Patricia knows things. Patricia has *opinions* about things. Patricia is currently, I can feel it, forming an opinion about this.

The four people who came in are queued behind Brittany, who is leaning her elbows on the counter as if she lives here. The first guy in line is checking his watch. The second person, a woman in scrubs, is doing the slow patient breathing of someone whose break is shorter than this is going to take. Behind her, two college kids are pretending to look at the book exchange shelf, but they are pretending poorly.

The milk has gone slightly past the temperature I want. I'm going to have to redo it, and I am going to have to redo it while she is still talking, which means I am going to nod at her about Biscuit's teeth while internally yelling at the steam wand.

My mother's voice arrives uninvited. *People are people, Rena. You listen. You don't make a fuss. You don't act like your time is more important than theirs.*

I have been getting better at hearing this voice and not flinching. I hear it. I keep working.

But underneath the mother-voice, today, there's something else. Something newer and quieter. It is — and I want to be clear that this is going to sound stupid — it is the voice of a woman I have never met, a woman in scrubs in a coffee shop line, who needs a coffee and twelve minutes of sitting down before her next four hours of being a nurse, and who is currently being held hostage by a stranger's contractor drama.

I think the words *not right now*. I think them in my head three times in a row, like I'm trying them on.

*Not right now. Not right now. Not right now.*

The pitcher is in my hand. I am, at some point, going to have to set it down. I set it down. I set it down on the edge of the steam tray, not in the center, because half my brain is busy and the other half is composing a sentence I have never said in my life. The pitcher slides. Catches on the lip. Doesn't fall.[^1]

I do not notice this happening, which is perhaps a record for me.

Brittany has now moved on to the contractor's *assistant*, who apparently is also part of the problem.

I take a breath. I look up. I make eye contact, which I have been working on. I say:

"Brittany — I'm gonna stop you for just a second."

The whole shop hears it. I hear my voice in my own ears, and it sounds like somebody else's voice, somebody who has done this before, somebody calm. I do not feel calm. I feel like I am about to spontaneously combust into a small pile of guilt.

"I have customers waiting, and I really do want to hear you — but I can't actually hear you and run the bar at the same time. Could you grab the corner table? I'll bring you a refill in about fifteen minutes when this line clears, and we can talk then."

It comes out so much gentler than I'd been afraid it would. I had been afraid it would come out the way my mother's no came out — flat, final, with the implication that you'd been bad. This came out like an offer. Like a chair being pulled out for someone.

There is a half-second where I genuinely don't know what's about to happen. Brittany's face does several things at once. I have *kicked her out of a conversation*. In the version of myself I grew up being, this is a sin somewhere between gossip and divorce.

Then she says, "Oh."

She says it the way you say it when something has been pointed out that you knew already.

"Oh — yeah. Of course. *God*, of course. I'm sorry. I do this. I'm — I'll be over there." She gestures at the corner. She picks up her latte. She looks at me, and her smile is small and a little embarrassed and entirely real. "Thank you. Sorry."

She goes.

*I have just done a thing.*

*I have just done a thing my mother told me women do not do.*

The woman in scrubs steps forward. She does not say anything about Brittany. She just orders an Americano, with a tiny upward twitch at the corner of her mouth that I'm choosing to interpret as solidarity. I pull her shots. I make her Americano. I work the line. I am absolutely positive that I am about to throw up out of pure social adrenaline,[^2] but my hands keep working, the way they always do.

When the line clears, I look across the room.

Patricia is watching me. Patricia, who once chewed me out for ninety seconds about foam-to-espresso ratio, and who has been almost unbearably gentle with me lately, like she's saving up her sharp edges for somebody else now.

Patricia gives me the smallest nod. Just a nod. Almost nothing.

I almost cry into the espresso machine.

---

Fifteen minutes later, I bring Brittany a refill.

She has been on her phone the whole time. Not scrolling — typing. Her face is calmer than when she came in, the way faces look when somebody has been doing the slow work of catching up to themselves.

I set the latte down. I'm braced for the conversation to resume where we left it — the contractor, the sister-in-law, Biscuit and his fourteen-hundred-dollar teeth. I have made my peace with this. I told her I'd come back, and I came back, and now I will hear about all of it.

Instead, she looks up.

"My therapist says I trauma-dump," she says. "I'm working on it. Thank you for the boundary."

I have been holding the latte saucer with both hands, which is fortunate, because I almost drop it anyway.

I have, in my entire life, been thanked for many things. I have been thanked for coffee. I have been thanked for opening early. I have not, ever, in thirty-some years of being a person, been thanked, in plain English, by a stranger, for *the boundary.*

The word sits there on the table next to the latte like a thing she's just handed me.

"Oh," I say. Eloquence. Iconic.

"I'm in therapy," Brittany says. "Started two months ago. She's been on me about — this. The thing I was just doing. To you. I unload. And then I feel terrible afterward. And then I do it again. So. Yeah. *Thank you.* That was — yeah. That was really kind."

"It didn't feel kind," I say, before I can stop myself. "It felt like the worst thing I've ever done."

She laughs. It's a wet laugh, a tired laugh, but a real one.

"That's how you know it was right," she says. "She tells me that too. My therapist."

I look at her, this woman whose name I learned eighteen minutes ago, who has just handed me a piece of theology I have been trying to pry out of Scripture for months. *Let your yes be yes and your no be no.* Mother had used that verse to mean *don't waver.* I had always read it as *don't disappoint.* Brittany's therapist, apparently, reads it as *don't lie about how much you have to give.*

That's a better reading. I make a small mental note to tell nobody about this, because the moment I tell anybody it'll start being a sermon and not a thing I learned.

"I'll bring you a water," I say. "On the house."

"You don't have to."

"I know," I say. And it comes out easy, the way *not right now* came out easy, like a muscle I didn't know I had has been there the whole time, getting stronger in the dark.

I bring her a water. She thanks me again, less for the water than for the whole afternoon. She sits there for another forty minutes, mostly quiet, mostly typing on her phone with her latte going cold beside her, and when she leaves, the bell rings, and Patricia, in the corner, lifts her own cup like she's making a toast to absolutely nothing.

I pick up the milk pitcher. I notice, finally, that it's been balanced on the edge of the steam tray for the better part of an hour, and that if I had bumped the counter at any point this whole story would have ended very differently.

I move it.

I refill the sugar caddy. The sugar caddy, predictably, dumps itself onto the counter the moment I turn around.

Some things don't change.

But — and I want to be careful here, because I'm new at this — some things, apparently, do.
